Passive Motion Localization

This Owl Platform solver uses RSSI variance data, combined with the locations of transmitters and receivers, to determine the location of motion within an area of interest.

Tracking without Training

The solver is both an ongoing research project and a solver for the Owl Platform. The primary goal is to obtain device-free motion localization for "free" in situations where device (active) localization is already possible. By free I mean that there shouldn't be any additional set-up or data collection required beyond what is necessary for active localization.

The current version works on a very simple principle: as objects move around in an RF-rich environment, they can cause interference with the radio waves. This interference is observable by receivers in the form of RSSI (Received Signal Strength Indicator) fluctuations. By computing the short-term (3-5 second) variance of the RSSI value, and knowing the location of some transmitters and receivers (already necessary for active localization), it is possible to approximate the location of the interfering motion using a simple lines-intersecting-tiles technique.

The Algorithm

Indoor RF propagation paths are far from simple. In fact, multiple-path propagation effects make communication, tracking, and other radio-based technologies much more difficult than outdoor or in "free space". However, in many indoor environments, two general properties can be observed: (1) the human body strongly influences the propagation of RF signals in the 1-2.4GHz range, and (2) line-of-sight signal propagation estimation is slightly inaccurate, but very inexpensive.

To exploit these two observations, the algorithm works like this:

  1. Partition the space into an evenly-sized grid of rectangular "tiles". Each tile will overlap its neighboring tiles by 50% (to somewhat reduce aliasing effects).
  2. Compute the standard deviation of the RSSI value from every transmitter to every receiver where the link distance is ≤30m. Trimming long links helps to elminate variances caused by random effects.
  3. Compute a "score" for each tile based on the value of each link that passes over it. The link is assumed to follow a straight path between the locations of the transmitter and receiver. Details are not provided here, but are available in the source code.
  4. Over the entire set of tiles, identify those tiles whose score is a local maximum and filter based on a minimum threshold.
  5. The tiles that remain are predictions of the location of motion in the area.

While the algorithm is rather crude and simplistic, it works surprisingly well. Without needing to train the algorithm for specific environmental conditions, experimental results showed that it could achieve 90% correct localization of motion for up to 3 individuals with reasonable accuracy and low false positive rates.

Continuing Work

As it is a research project, and a bit of a hobby of mine, I continue to work on improving the results and trying new ideas. Currently I'm working with convolution matrices to try and reduce the area identifying the motion without sacrificing the accuracy or recall.
